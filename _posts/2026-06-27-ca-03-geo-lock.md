CA-02 covered the three essential policies every tenant needs. This one adds the next layer: location-based blocking — or as most admins call it, geo lock.

The idea is simple. If your organisation operates in New Zealand and has no staff, vendors, or travel to large parts of the world, why is your tenant accepting sign-in attempts from everywhere on the planet?

## The Logic

> **IF** a sign-in comes from a country you never operate in → **THEN** block it

Two things need to exist for this to work: **named locations** (the country lists) and a **CA policy** that references them.

## Step 1 — Build the Named Location

Under **Entra ID → Conditional Access → Named locations**:

- **Countries location** — create a named location, tick the countries you want to treat as blocked (or the ones you allow, depending on your policy design)
- **Determine location by** — two options: IP address (GeoIP lookup) or GPS coordinates. IP-based is standard; GPS requires the Authenticator app and is mainly for high-assurance scenarios
- **Include unknown countries/regions** — decide whether IPs that can't be mapped to a country get caught too. Attackers use anonymising infrastructure precisely because it's hard to attribute — I'd include unknowns in the block and handle any legitimate edge cases with exclusions

You'll also want a second named location: **your trusted egress IPs** (office ranges, VPN concentrators), marked as trusted. It's useful in other policies too.

## Step 2 — Two Ways to Design the Policy

### Option A — Block List

Block the specific countries you choose; allow everything else.

- Easier to introduce, low friction
- But it's reactive — you're maintaining a list of "bad" countries forever, and attackers just route through whichever country you didn't list

### Option B — Allow List

Block **all** countries except the ones you explicitly operate in.

- Far stronger default — new attack origins are blocked automatically
- Needs more care: staff travel, overseas vendors, and cloud-hosted services with foreign egress IPs all need thinking through

For a NZ-based org, an allow list of NZ (plus wherever staff genuinely travel or vendors connect from) is usually the right call. Start with the block list only if you need a gentle first step.

## Step 3 — The Policy Itself

- **Users** — all users, **excluding break-glass accounts** (same rule as every CA policy — if this policy misfires, you still need a way in)
- **Target resources** — all cloud apps
- **Conditions → Locations** — include the blocked-countries named location (or "any location" with your allowed countries excluded, for the allow-list design)
- **Grant** — Block access
- **Report-only first** — same advice as CA-02. Run it in report-only for at least a week and check the sign-in logs for what *would* have been blocked. Staff personal travel and third-party services show up here, and it's much better to find them in a report than in a after hour callout

## Be Honest About What This Is

Geo blocking is a **noise filter, not a security boundary**. Worth being upfront about the limits:

- **VPNs defeat it trivially** — any attacker targeting you specifically will connect from a NZ residential proxy and sail through - bad guys need additional effort
- **GeoIP isn't perfect** — IP-to-country mapping has errors, especially around mobile carriers and cloud provider ranges (I have experienced with starlink ips)
- **It does nothing against a compromised device inside an allowed country**

So why? Because the overwhelming majority of credential stuffing and password spray traffic is untargeted, high-volume, and comes from infrastructure that doesn't bother masking its origin. Geo lock removes that noise from your sign-in logs — which makes the attacks that *do* matter far easier to see. Cleaner logs are a genuine security win, just not the one this policy is usually sold as.

## Common Mistakes

- **Forgetting break-glass exclusions** — then a GeoIP mis-mapping locks out your emergency account along with everything else
- **Blocking countries your SaaS vendors egress from** — that third-party integration signing in from a US or Singapore datacentre will hit the block. Report-only mode catches this (if you operate from nz and you might need Aus allowed for SaaS flow)
- **Set-and-forget** — staff travel changes, vendors change. Review the allowed list on a schedule, not never

## Quick Reference

| Decision | Recommendation |
|---|---|
| Block list vs allow list | Allow list, if you can manage the exceptions |
| Unknown countries/regions | Include in the block |
| Break-glass accounts | Always excluded |
| Rollout | Report-only for at least a week first |
| Review cadence | Revisit allowed countries quarterly |
